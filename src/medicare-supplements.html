Medicare Supplement (Medigap) plans, explained.

Original Medicare covers a lot — but not all — of your medical costs. Medigap plans help pay the deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance left behind, so a hospital stay or major treatment doesn’t become a financial emergency.

How Medigap works alongside Original Medicare

A Medicare Supplement plan is sold by a private insurer and works alongside — not instead of — Original Medicare Part A and Part B. Medicare pays its share of approved costs first, then your Medigap plan pays some or all of the remainder, depending on the plan you choose.

Standardized lettered plans

Medigap plans are standardized by the federal government, which means a Plan G from one company covers the same benefits as a Plan G from another — the difference is usually price and customer service. Common plans include Plan G and Plan N, which are the most popular options among people newly eligible for Medicare, since Plans C and F are no longer available to those who became eligible on or after January 1, 2020.

  • Plan G: Covers nearly everything Original Medicare doesn’t, aside from the annual Part B deductible.
  • Plan N: Similar to Plan G but with small copays for some office and ER visits, usually at a lower monthly premium.
  • Other lettered plans (A, B, D, K, L, M): Offer varying levels of coverage — we can walk you through which still make sense for your situation.

Your Medigap Open Enrollment Period

Your best window to buy a Medigap policy is the 6-month period that starts the month you're both 65 or older and enrolled in Part B. During this window, insurers must sell you a policy regardless of health conditions. Outside that window, you may be subject to medical underwriting in most states.

Frequently asked questions

Can I have a Medigap plan and a Medicare Advantage plan at the same time?
No — it's illegal for someone to sell you a Medigap policy if they know you have a Medicare Advantage plan, since the two aren't meant to work together.
Do Medigap plans include prescription drug coverage?
No. Medigap plans sold today do not include drug coverage, so you'll want to pair one with a standalone Part D plan.
Can I switch Medigap plans or carriers later?
Yes, but outside your initial Open Enrollment Period or a guaranteed-issue situation, the new insurer can typically ask health questions and may decline coverage or adjust pricing based on your health.
Are Medigap premiums the same everywhere?
No. Since the benefits are standardized but pricing isn't, premiums for the same lettered plan can vary significantly between insurance companies — which is exactly why comparing carriers matters.
